Praveen: Let's have a little chat about this sentence: "Sir, sir, please drink from up, sir!"
That sentence is a classic example of "Indian English" used to explain a unique local drinking habit. It refers to the practice of pouring liquid into your mouth from a distance without letting the container touch your lips.

Why People Do This

  • Hygiene: It allows multiple people to share one water bottle safely.
  • Purity: Traditional customs place high value on avoiding saliva contamination (called jootha or uchistha).
  • Convenience: It eliminates the need to wash cups constantly in public spaces.

Language Breakdown

  • The "Up" Concept: Native English speakers usually say "pour it in" or "don't touch the rim." "Drinking from up" is a direct translation of Hindi phrases like uupar se peena.
  • The Triple "Sir": Repeating titles shows extreme politeness, urgency, or customer service anxiety.
  • Grammar Shift: Standard English requires an object (e.g., "drink it from above"), but this localized version drops the object entirely.

The Logic of "Literalism"

  • Humans naturally translate the physical mechanics of their native language into a new one.
  • Uupar se perfectly describes the vertical distance of the bottle, so "from up" becomes the most efficient English substitute.
  • It prioritizes speed of concept over formal grammatical rules.

The Mechanics of Politeness

  • In many languages, respect is shown through repetition and tone, not complex sentence structures.
  • Saying "sir" three times replaces the missing grammatical fluff with pure, urgent deference.
  • It ensures the listener pays attention immediately without causing offense.
